Overview

There are currently two different kinds of approaches to internet governance. The current style of governance is called the multi-stakeholder approach. It Is a consensus-based approach that gives equal footing to three different pillars: government, industry and civil society. Within civil society, however, there is a special distinction for the internet technical community, which is composed of the engineers responsible for internet standards coming into existence.  (The Internet Society represents this technical perspective).

The second style of governance is called the traditional or multilateral approach. A multilateral approach to governance is typically used for telecommunications, not internet, issues. For instance, the International Telecommunications Union (ITU) is a UN agency composed of member states, which have signed on, and other private organizations. The ITU allows governments and the private sector to coordinate about global telecommunication networks and services.

The multi-stakeholder and multilateral approaches provide two different ways of creating policy statements on an international level for policy related issues. The goal of this activity is to model the decision-making processes under each approach. 

Your task is to work in a group to generate a short statement of principles to provide guidance for policymakers, industry, and internet users worldwide on the issue of authentication and online identity.

As a starting point for discussion, a draft of a statement of principles has been circulated, based on the U.S. National Strategy for Trusted Identities in Cyberspace (NSTIC).  The full NSTIC strategy document is available here. PDF. 

The draft statement of principles is as follows.

Draft Policy Statement on Identity Management:

Individuals and organizations utilize secure, efficient, easy-to-use, and interoperable identity solutions to access online services in a manner that promotes confidence, privacy, choice, and innovation.

  1. Identity solutions will be privacy-enhancing and voluntary
  2. Identity solutions will be secure and resilient
  3. Identity solutions will be interoperable
  4. Identity solutions will be cost-effective and easy to use

Group 1 Instructions (Click to Expand)

Group 1 will model the multi-stakeholder approach. Students within this group are assigned to represent a country, company or a civil society group.  One of you will be assigned the job of secretary.  You should think about how the entity you represent is likely to think about the question of online identity, although you are not bound by any specific instructions.

Please use this discussion page for this exercise. This discussion platform will allow you to converse with your group members but will not be visible to the entire class. Conversations will be visible to the instructor.  The entire exercise must wrap up by end of day Saturday. 

1).  Post your initial position.  First, introduce yourself by describing your constituency (country, company or civil society group) and your perspective on the topic of authentication and online identity.  Second, offer suggestions for deletions, modifications, or additions to the draft statement above, or offer reasons for accepting the statement as it is written. Please provide your initial post as early as possible, and by no later than midnight on Friday.

2).  Seeking rough consensus.  Members should indicate support by liking suggested changes, and should express disagreement by commenting on suggested changes.  You may hum if you like, but no one will hear you. :).  You may begin the discussion (by liking and posting replies) as early as you like and please wrap up by no later than Saturday noon if possible.  

There will be no formal voting. Participants need not agree with everything because the purpose of the exercise is find rough consensus.  Members should refrain from offering suggestions they expect will generate sharp disagreement from other members.

3).  Resolution.  The revised draft statement will consist of the original, draft statement, plus any deletions, modifications or additions that have received support from a large majority of the group (2/3rds or more) and have not drawn any sharp disagreements from any other members.

By Saturday at 5pm (or earlier, if possible), the Secretary will post a final draft of the text to the group for review. At this stage, participants may dissent if they continue to have sharp disagreements with the statement. (Note that a failure to dissent does not mean the participant agrees with everything in the statement.)

A dissent from 3 or fewer participants will be considered "rough consensus." Dissents should be posted no later than midnight on Saturday.  Participants should seek to avoid dissents as much as possible. 

The final statement, if one achieves "rough consensus," will be posted by the Secretary on the next page in this module titled "Discussion: Approaches to Decision-Making."  The final statement, or a statement saying that the group failed to achieve rough consensus, should be posted by the Secretary as early as possible after Saturday at midnight, and no later than Sunday at 10am.

Group 2 Instructions (Click to Expand)

The remaining third of the class will take part in modeling the multilateral approach.  The multilateral decision-making group models a more formal approach to decision-making.  Votes will be taken, and majority rules. Students in this group will represent their assigned countries. You should advance your particular country's views on online identity as faithfully as possible.

Please use this discussion page to collaborate and share the point of view from your assumed role. This discussion platform will allow you to converse with your group members but will not be visible to the entire class. Conversations will be visible to the instructor. The exercise must wrap up by end of day Saturday.

One of you will be assigned to act as the Chairman.

1. Post your initial position.  First, describe your nation's perspective on the topic of authentication and online identity and your position on the draft statement (support, support with modifications, or oppose).  Second, offer suggestions for deletions, modifications, or additions to the draft statement above, or offer reasons for accepting the statement as it is written. Please provide your initial post as soon as possible, but no later than midnight on Friday. 

2. Debate. Members should indicate support by voting up ('liking') suggested changes, and should express disagreement by commenting on suggested changes.  Please begin the debate as soon as possible, and complete debate by no later than noon on Saturday.  The Chairman will revise the statement to incorporate any changes that have received majority support from the members, and will post the revised statement by no later than 5pm on Saturday.  

3. Vote. Before midnight on Saturday, please indicate your vote in favor of the revised statement by "liking" the Chairman's post, and your vote (or abstaining) by replying to the Chairman's post.  While a majority is sufficient, representatives should aim for a statement that is as close to unanimous as possible. 

After midnight on Saturday and no later than 10am on Sunday, the chairman will post the final statement, if one achieves a majority, or a statement saying that the group was unable to agree, on the next page in this module titled "Discussion: Approaches to Decision-Making."  The final vote will also be posted.

 

Final Instructions for Both Groups

By end of day Saturday, each group should wrap up their discussion. The Secretary and Chairman of the groups should then post the results of the different decision making processing on the next page of the Module, titled "Discussion: Approaches to Decision-Making." Further instructions on that page.
